What is DISC?

Decision Integration for Strong Communities (DISC)

×
The intersection where the natural, built and social environments meet is where a "place" becomes a "home." As a community changes, challenges arise that may threaten the quality of life characteristics you value most. From creating jobs to building greener infrastructure, many considerations are needed to identify local investment options that may prove most beneficial to your community over time. Assessing the status of those characteristics and the local amenities that can influence them is often the first step toward finding solutions to resolve community issues. Indicators and indices can be used to assess various community attributes to help inform the local decision-making process. Indicators

The Decision Integration for Strong Communities (DISC) application integrates raw measures (e.g., median income, disease prevalence) to help you interpret patterns observed in the data. Resulting indicators are presented to characterize qualities that represent or support vitality, health, resilience, and well-being in your community. DISC features allow you to incorporate local knowledge or data to reflect local conditions better. You have an option to build decision-case scenarios to help inform decision making discussions. Finally, DISC offers resource information that may help you on your journey toward creating a sustainable and robust community.

DISC Score

The Decision Integration for Strong Communities (DISC) index is a summary measure that provides a profile about select community characteristics. The index is based on indicators and metrics derived from existing cultural, economic, health, and other related data. The DISC profile index provides a snapshot about quality of life characteristics common across all communities and is sensitive to changes in the economic, social and ecological environments.

Community Characteristic Scores



Well-being and resilience measures are often used to characterize the drivers that influence human behavior and affect the human condition. These quality of life attributes are multi-dimensional that are best described using a host of metrics that respond to the changing environment in which people live. A synthesis of this knowledge helps shape our understanding about people and their needs, both actual and perceived.

Services

There exists a myriad of currencies that frame the underpinnings of a thriving society-not all are dollars and cents. The wants and needs of people are met through the availability of items (i.e., goods) and delivery of assistance (i.e., services). The availability and flow of economic, social and ecosystem services serve many purposes within a community-everything from health to employment to clean water. New investment in or redistribution of economic, social and natural resources is the conduit for introducing change that influence quality of life outcomes.
